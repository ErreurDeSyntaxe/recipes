Against Death

In Meditations, Marcus Aurelius reflects on death as an inevitable and natural part of the human experience. He contemplates the transient nature of life, emphasizing that death is a universal fate that everyone must face. Rather than fearing death, Marcus Aurelius encourages a stoic acceptance of its inevitability, viewing it as a natural process in the cycle of existence.



He underscores the idea that dwelling on the fear of death is counterproductive and distracts from living a virtuous and meaningful life. Instead, he suggests focusing on the present moment, acting with integrity, and fulfilling one's responsibilities. For Marcus Aurelius, the acceptance of mortality becomes a catalyst for a life well-lived, urging individuals to make the most of their time and contribute positively to the world.

Ingredients

  • Knowledge that all must die
  • Desire to live responsibily and meaningfully
  • Wisdom
  • Courage
  • Temperance
  • Justice

Steps

  1. Be born.
  2. Observe death of plants, animals, and perhaps even stars.
  3. Acknowledge the inevitability of death.
  4. Consider the weight and uselessness of the fear of death.
  5. Cast aside the fear of death.
  6. Live a meaningful, virtuous, and responsible life.
  7. Fulfill your last duty: die.
